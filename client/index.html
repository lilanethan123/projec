Germany 1918 - 1945

An exploration of the rise of totalitarianism, the primary witnesses of the era, and the global response.

The Rise to Power

Adolf Hitler, named the “Fuhrer” (leader), was born in April 1889 in Braunau am Inn. After serving in WWI, he found his voice in the DAP (German Workers' Party), eventually transforming it into the National Socialist German Workers' Party.

Through powerful oratory and aggressive political maneuvering, he rose from an unofficial leader in 1921 to the indefinite leader of Germany by 1933. His promotion was fueled by a promise to restore German pride after the perceived humiliation of the Great War.

Primary Witnesses

Winston Churchill

The British leader who watched closely as the shadow lengthened. While others hoped for peace, Churchill warned that the fascist government was "very dangerous," describing Nazi aggression as a threat to global stability long before the first shots were fired.

Joseph Goebbels

The architect of propaganda. He viewed the press as a "keyboard" for the government. His machinery of persuasion utilized cognitive biases and emotional responses to sway an entire nation, believing that "the moment one becomes aware of propaganda, it loses its effectiveness."

"Think of the press as a great keyboard on which the government can play." - Joseph Goebbels

Dr. Josef Mengele

Infamously known as the "Angel of Death," Mengele was a physician at Auschwitz who performed horrific "scientific" studies on prisoners. He was responsible for the selection process, deciding who was forced into the gas chambers.

FDR & Isolationism

Across the Atlantic, Franklin D. Roosevelt faced a nation deeply entrenched in isolationism. The trauma of WWI had left Americans wary of foreign entanglements, leading to an age of political and social detachment from European conflicts.

Despite this, FDR worked to prepare the "Arsenal of Democracy," navigating a complex political landscape where the threat of totalitarianism grew stronger every day.
